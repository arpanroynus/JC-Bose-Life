Stage 2: Class 8 Level
Formation and Characteristics

Volcanic soil, also known as Regur or Black Cotton Soil, is formed in the Deccan Trap region of India. It originates from the weathering of igneous rocks (basalt) that were formed by ancient volcanic activity. These soils are extremely fine-textured and clayey in nature.

One unique feature of this soil is its ability to retain moisture for a long time. During the dry season, it develops deep cracks, which helps in "self-plowing" by allowing oxygen to reach the deeper layers. It is rich in nutrients like calcium carbonate, magnesium, and potash, though it typically lacks phosphorus.

Stage 3: Class 10 Mastery
Agricultural & Chemical Significance

In the ICSE curriculum, Black Soil is categorized by its high argillaceous (clayey) content. Chemically, it is composed of iron, lime, aluminum, and magnesium. The dark color is attributed to the presence of titaniferous magnetite or small amounts of iron and aluminum silicate. It is found predominantly in Maharashtra, Gujarat, and parts of Madhya Pradesh.

From an agricultural standpoint, this soil is synonymous with the "Cotton Belt of India." Because of its high moisture-retention capacity, it is ideal for dry farming. However, when wet, the soil becomes extremely sticky and difficult to manage, requiring farmers to till the land immediately after the first monsoon shower.

The "Self-Plowing" phenomenon mentioned in earlier stages is critical for the O-Level/ICSE board exams. These deep fissures allow for deep aeration of the soil. You must remember that while it is fertile for cotton, citrus fruits, and tobacco, it is deficient in Nitrogen and Organic matter (Humus), often requiring specific fertilizers for high-yield farming.
